Putting Butts in Seats

The NBA has a total of 30 teams. Monetary valuations of each franchise range from $750 million to $3 billion, with the New Orleans Pelicans being the lowest valued, and the New York Knicks being the highest valued. When it comes to arena capacity and fan attendance, values ranged from 72.4% of capacity to 103.6% of capacity. Looking at the visualization we can see that nearly all of the highest valued teams($1+ billion) filled 99.2% or more of seats. Of the teams valued under $1 billion, the Utah Jazz did the best job of filling the seats at 98.8%. After the Jazz, attendance and valuation generally start to drop off equally. There are a few outliers, namely the Brooklyn Nets, and Houston Rockets. Both franchises are worth in excess of $1.5 billion, yet in the bottom half of attendance, with the Nets only filling 85.2% of their seats.

This visualization does show that the highest valued franchises do indeed have the highest average attendance. To get an answer as to why, we would need to dig deeper. So many factors go into attendance, such as franchise market, star players, franchise history, ticket prices, the arena itself, and most importantly, team performance. One example would be to look at the Dallas Mavericks who averaged 103.1% of capacity, and the Houston Rockets who were at only 94.1% of capacity. A safe assumption would be the Mavericks had a good record, and the Rockets had a bad record. In this case, the opposite is true because the Mavericks were one of the worst teams in the league that season, and Rockets were one of the best. Do the Rockets play a boring style of basketball? Do they have no star players? Ultimately more data would need to be analyzed to answer these, and similar questions.
